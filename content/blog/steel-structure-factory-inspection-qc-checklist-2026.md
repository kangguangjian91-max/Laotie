# Steel Structure Factory Inspection: QC Checklist 2026

Importing a steel structure from overseas is a six-figure decision. The single most important thing you can do to protect that investment — beyond choosing a certified supplier — is a thorough factory inspection before your steel leaves China.

This guide covers what to check, when to inspect, and how to verify that your **steel structure quality control** standards are being met. Whether you visit in person, send a third-party inspector, or use video verification, this checklist ensures nothing is missed.

## Why Factory Inspection Matters

Consider the cost of discovering quality issues after your steel arrives at site:

| Problem | On-site Consequence | Estimated Cost |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| Wrong steel grade | Structural failure risk, engineering re-review | $5,000–20,000 |
| Poor welds (cracks, porosity) | Rejection by local engineer, re-welding on site | $3,000–15,000 |
| Incorrect bolt hole positions | Components won't fit, on-site drilling delays | $2,000–8,000 |
| Inadequate coating thickness | Premature corrosion, repaint within 3–5 years | $10,000–30,000 |
| Wrong dimensions | Rework or re-order | $10,000–50,000+ |

A $1,500–2,500 third-party inspection catches these issues before shipping — delivering a 10× to 50× return on investment. This is why experienced buyers never skip **steel structure factory inspection**.

## When to Inspect: The 4-Stage QC Model

Most buyers think of inspection as a single pre-shipment event. The best practice is a four-stage approach:

### Stage 1: Pre-Production Review (Week 1)
**What to verify before any steel is cut:**
- Shop drawings match your approved design
- Steel grade confirmed (Q235B, Q355B, S355, A992, etc.)
- Welding Procedure Specifications (WPS) qualified for your steel grades
- Coating specification documented (type, brand, DFT target)
- Production schedule confirmed

> At this stage, you also verify the factory is a legitimate **CE ISO certified steel structure** facility. Ask to see original certificates — not photocopies — and cross-check validity dates. ISO 9001 certificates expire after 3 years; CE (EN 1090) certification requires annual surveillance audits.

### Stage 2: In-Process Inspection (Weeks 2–3)
**Check during active fabrication:**
- CNC cutting accuracy on first-cut components (±1mm for critical dimensions)
- Weld quality on first batch of H-beams (visual + UT sampling)
- Welder qualifications — verify certificates match the workers on the line
- Fit-up accuracy before full welding

This is also when you verify that the **Chinese steel structure supplier** has allocated the production capacity promised. Walk the factory floor — are your components actually being processed on the lines assigned to your order?

### Stage 3: Pre-Shipment Inspection (Week 4–5)
**The most critical stage. Cover all 6 points below in detail.**

### Stage 4: Container Loading Inspection
**Verify before the container doors close:**
- Correct components loaded per packing list
- Proper bracing and protection (no steel-on-steel contact)
- Marking clearly visible on every component
- Container seal number recorded and photographed

## Stage 3 Deep Dive: The Pre-Shipment QC Checklist

This is the inspection that prevents bad steel from leaving the factory. Here's exactly what to check:

### 1. Material Verification

| Check | Method | Acceptance Criteria |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Steel grade confirmation | Review Mill Test Certificates (EN 10204 3.1) | Grade matches order (Q235B, Q355B, etc.) |
| Material thickness | Ultrasonic thickness gauge on random samples | ±0.3mm of specified thickness |
| Chemical composition | Cross-check MTR against standard | C ≤ 0.20%, Mn ≤ 1.50% (for Q355B) |
| Heat number traceability | Verify heat numbers stamped on components match MTRs | 100% match |

> **Red flag**: If the supplier cannot produce original MTRs with heat numbers matching the physical steel, stop the inspection. This is non-negotiable for **steel structure quality control**.

### 2. Dimensional Inspection

| Component | Check Points | Tolerance |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Column length | Overall length, bolt hole positions | ±2mm |
| Rafter length | Overall, splice locations | ±3mm |
| Beam straightness | At mid-span, both axes | L/1000, max 5mm |
| Web-to-flange perpendicularity | 3 points per member | 90° ± 1° |
| Bolt hole diameter | Go/no-go gauge | +0.5mm of bolt diameter |
| Bolt hole positions | Center-to-center, edge distance | ±1mm |
| Base plate flatness | Diagonal check | ±2mm |

**Sampling**: Inspect 100% of columns (they're critical for plumb) and 20% of other members. If any component fails, increase sampling to 50%.

### 3. Welding Quality

| Check | Method | Acceptance Criteria | Sampling |
|-------|--------|-------------------|----------|
| Weld surface appearance | Visual (VT) — 100% of welds | No cracks, undercut ≤ 0.5mm, smooth profile | 100% |
| Internal defects | Ultrasonic Testing (UT) | No indications exceeding reference level | 10% of butt welds |
| Surface cracks | Magnetic Particle Testing (MT) | No linear indications | 5% of fillet welds |
| Weld size | Fillet gauge | Leg length ≥ specified (typically 6–8mm minimum) | 20% |
| Full penetration verification | UT or RT for specified CJP welds | Complete fusion, no lack of penetration | 100% of CJP |

**Priority welds to test**: Column-to-base-plate, rafter apex connections, crane beam splices, and any full-penetration joint specified on drawings.

### 4. Surface Preparation and Coating

| Check | Method | Acceptance Criteria |
|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Blast cleanliness | Visual comparison to ISO 8501-1 | SA 2.5 (near-white metal) minimum |
| Surface profile | Testex tape or profile gauge | 50–75μm for epoxy systems |
| Primer DFT | Electronic DFT gauge (5 readings per member, 10 members min.) | 60–80μm (as specified) |
| Intermediate coat DFT | Same method | Per specification (typically 80–120μm) |
| Total DFT | Sum of all coats | ≥ specified value (typically 200–280μm) |
| Coating continuity | Holiday detector (sponge type, 67.5V) | No pinholes on 100% of critical areas |

### 5. Pre-Assembly / Trial Fit

For complex structures, insist on factory pre-assembly of at least one representative bay:

- Assemble columns, rafters, and bracing from one full bay
- Verify all bolt holes align without forcing
- Check that bracing fits within adjustment range
- Confirm base plate bolt pattern matches your foundation template
- Photograph the trial assembly — these photos are invaluable for your site crew

Pre-assembly catches hole misalignment and fabrication errors that would otherwise be discovered on site — when fixing them costs 5–10× more.

### 6. Marking and Documentation

Every component must be clearly marked with:
- **Piece mark** — matching the erection drawing (e.g., C1, R2A, B3)
- **Heat number** — traceable to MTR
- **Weight** — for lifting planning

Documentation to collect before release:
- ✅ Signed-off inspection report (this checklist)
- ✅ Mill Test Certificates (complete set, one per heat)
- ✅ Welder qualification certificates
- ✅ NDT reports (UT, MT, RT results)
- ✅ Coating inspection report with DFT readings
- ✅ Pre-assembly report (if performed)

## Red Flags: When to Reject a Shipment

Stop the shipment if you find:

1. **Steel grade mismatch** — MTR says Q355B but UT hardness test suggests Q235B
2. **Systematic weld defects** — cracks, lack of fusion, or excessive porosity on >5% of tested welds
3. **DFT below 60% of specification** — indicates rushed or skipped coating
4. **Bolt holes misaligned** — pre-assembly reveals components don't fit
5. **Supplier refuses inspection access** — the biggest red flag of all

A professional **Chinese steel structure supplier** welcomes inspection. If a factory makes excuses — "the inspector can't access the welding bay" or "we only allow inspection after packing" — walk away. No legitimate factory hides its production.
