# Steel Warehouse Design Guide: Spans, Crane Systems & Cladding Options

Designing a steel warehouse involves more than just choosing a size. The right design decisions — span configuration, crane integration, cladding selection — directly impact your building's functionality, cost, and long-term value. This guide covers the key design parameters every buyer should understand.

## 1. Span Configuration: Clear Span vs Multi-Span

The most fundamental design decision is how to configure the building's width.

### Clear Span (Single Bay)

A single portal frame spanning the full width with no interior columns.

| Span | Typical Use | Frame Weight |
|---|---|---|
| 12–18m | Small workshops, storage sheds | Light |
| 20–30m | Standard warehouses | Medium |
| 30–40m | Large distribution centers | Heavy |
| 40–50m+ | Sports halls, exhibition spaces | Very heavy (custom) |

**Pros**: Unobstructed floor space, flexible layout, fast forklift movement
**Cons**: Heavier frame per m² for spans over 30m

### Multi-Span (Multiple Bays)

Two or more portal frames side-by-side with interior columns on a shared grid.

**Pros**: Lighter frame per m², lower steel cost for wide buildings
**Cons**: Interior columns reduce layout flexibility

**Rule of thumb**: For buildings wider than 40m, multi-span is typically more economical. A 60m wide building as 2 × 30m spans saves approximately 15–20% in frame steel weight compared to a single 60m span.

## 2. Crane Systems

Integrating an overhead crane requires specific design considerations.

### Crane Types for Steel Buildings

| Crane Type | Capacity | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Underhung crane** | 1–10 tons | Light manufacturing, small workshops |
| **Single girder overhead** | 5–20 tons | General warehouse, medium duty |
| **Double girder overhead** | 10–100 tons | Heavy industry, steel fabrication |
| **Gantry crane** | 1–20 tons | Outdoor yards, low-height buildings |

### Design Implications

Adding a crane system affects the building design significantly:

- **Crane columns**: Must be heavier (typically H-section, 300–600mm depth)
- **Crane beams**: Add 10–20% to steel weight for the main frame
- **Eave height**: Must accommodate: hook height + crane height + clearance (typically add 2–3m)
- **Column base**: Larger base plates and anchor bolts required
- **Foundation**: Crane columns need deeper foundations to resist uplift

### Cost Impact of Crane Systems
| Crane Capacity | Additional Cost (per m²) | Additional Steel Weight |
|---|---|---|
| 5 ton underhung | $3–5/m² | 3–5 kg/m² |
| 10 ton single girder | $6–10/m² | 5–8 kg/m² |
| 20 ton double girder | $10–18/m² | 8–15 kg/m² |
| 50 ton double girder | $20–35/m² | 15–25 kg/m² |

## 3. Eave Height

The eave height is measured from finished floor level to the underside of the roof beam at the column line.

| Eave Height | Typical Use |
|---|---|
| 4–6m | Small workshops, agricultural sheds |
| 6–8m | Standard storage warehouses |
| 8–10m | Distribution centers, light manufacturing |
| 10–12m | Heavy manufacturing, crane-served buildings |
| 12–20m | Specialized industrial (tall equipment) |

Increasing eave height by 1m adds approximately 3–5% to the frame steel weight.

## 4. Roof & Wall Cladding Options

| Cladding Type | U-Value | Cost/m² | Best Application |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Single-skin steel sheet** | 5.0–6.0 | $8–12 | Uninsulated warehouses, cost-sensitive |
| **Insulated sandwich panel (PIR)** | 0.22–0.28 | $18–28 | Temperature-controlled, cold storage |
| **Insulated sandwich panel (rock wool)** | 0.40–0.55 | $20–32 | Fire-rated buildings, factories |
| **Insulated panel (EPS)** | 0.30–0.45 | $14–22 | Dry applications, budget option |
| **Double-skin with insulation** | 0.35–0.50 | $16–24 | General insulated buildings |

For most applications, **PIR sandwich panels** offer the best balance of thermal performance, cost, and ease of installation.

## 5. Structural Optimization Tips

### Reduce steel weight without sacrificing strength:

1. **Use high-strength steel**: Q355B (S355JR) instead of Q235B saves 10–15% in weight
2. **Optimize bay spacing**: 7.5–9m bays are typically the most economical
3. **Consider tapered frames**: Variable-depth rafters match the bending moment diagram
4. **Use Z-purlin lapping**: Continuous span Z-purlins save 15–25% compared to simple span
5. **Design for local wind/snow**: Don't over-specify — use local code values, not generic loads
